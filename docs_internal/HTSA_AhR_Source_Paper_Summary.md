# HTSA source-paper summary — AhR restricts axon regeneration by balancing neuronal stress and growth response after injury

Date: 2026-04-15
Primary paper: *AhR restricts axon regeneration by balancing neuronal stress and growth response after injury* ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649))

## Citation / paper metadata

- **Title:** *AhR restricts axon regeneration by balancing neuronal stress and growth response after injury* ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649))
- **Authors:** Dalia Halawani and colleagues ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649))
- **Venue:** bioRxiv preprint ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649))
- **Core topic:** how AhR shapes the balance between neuronal stress adaptation and axon regrowth after injury, especially in DRG neurons ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649))

## Central question and hypothesis

The paper asks how injured neurons balance protective stress programs against the need to reactivate growth. The authors frame AhR as a candidate regulator of that balance because AhR belongs to the bHLH-PAS transcription-factor family alongside environmental and hypoxia-responsive sensors such as HIF-1α and Bmal1, but its role in axonal injury had remained unclear ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)). Their working hypothesis is that AhR is not simply another pro-regeneration transcription factor, but a brake that restrains regrowth by enforcing a stress-preserving state after axotomy ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)).

## Model systems and injury context

The source paper uses adult mouse dorsal root ganglion (DRG) neurons as the main mechanistic model because DRG neurons mount a strong regenerative response after peripheral axotomy and therefore provide a tractable system for studying axon regrowth programs ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)). The study includes both peripheral nerve paradigms, especially sciatic nerve lesion / crush, and spinal cord injury paradigms to test whether AhR-dependent effects generalize beyond the peripheral setting ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)). For the RNA-seq arm relevant to our project, ipsilateral L4-L6 DRGs were compared against contralateral DRGs one day after peripheral lesion so the injured side could be interpreted against an internal side-matched control from the same animal ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)).

## Why DRG and why conditional knockout

DRGs matter in this paper because they let the authors study a neuron population that can regenerate after peripheral injury, while still connecting that biology to conditioning-lesion logic and to spinal cord injury relevance ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)). The paper uses neuronal conditional Ahr knockout models instead of treating constitutive knockout as the main design because constitutive AhR loss is associated with broader baseline phenotypes, including demyelination and inflammation, that would make it harder to argue for a neuron-intrinsic regeneration effect ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)). The paper also explicitly reports that enhanced regrowth in the conditional knockout was not explained by altered immune-cell or Schwann-cell responses at the injury site, strengthening the neuron-intrinsic interpretation ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)).

## Major mechanistic findings

### 1) AhR behaves as a neuronal brake on regeneration

The paper shows that ligand-mediated AhR signaling restrains neurite outgrowth, whereas genetic deletion or pharmacologic inhibition of AhR promotes axon growth and improves regenerative outcomes in vivo ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)). This is the cleanest single-sentence mechanism claim we can safely reuse in our paper.

### 2) AhR is induced in injured DRGs after peripheral lesion

After peripheral lesion, the source paper reports increased expression of Ahr, Arnt, and related pathway components in ipsilateral DRGs, with early post-injury induction confirmed by RNA-seq, qRT-PCR, and protein-level assays ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)). This matters for our framing because it supports the idea that AhR is part of the early injury-response program rather than only a baseline developmental regulator.

### 3) Injury status is stronger than genotype at the global transcriptome level

The paper’s own PCA result is especially important for our interpretation discipline: injury status explained the dominant variance, while genotype did not create a broad clean separation on its own ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)). That means our write-up should keep side-specific injury contrasts as the main story and treat genotype as a more selective or pathway-level modifier.

### 4) AhR supports stress-response and proteostasis programs after axotomy

The mechanistic transcriptomic story in the source paper is that AhR-dependent injury responses preferentially support proteostasis, translational restraint, and related stress-preserving programs after axotomy ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)). In comparative pathway analysis, control DRGs showed stronger proteostasis-related signatures, while Ahr cKO DRGs shifted toward biosynthesis, signaling, and growth-linked states ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)).

### 5) AhR loss redirects neurons toward translation, metabolism, and pro-growth signaling

Ahr cKO neurons showed increased de novo protein synthesis and transcriptional shifts involving translational control, mTOR-linked regulation, metabolism, and growth-promoting programs ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)). This is the basis for cautious language in our intro/discussion about translation and metabolism. We should still describe these as pathway-level themes rather than as settled causal endpoints in our own reanalysis.

### 6) HIF-1α is mechanistically relevant to the growth effect

The paper links the regenerative phenotype of AhR loss to HIF-1α-dependent signaling, arguing that the growth advantage requires HIF-1α activity and that AhR, HIF-1α, and ARNT interact within the broader stress-versus-growth regulatory network ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)). This supports mentioning HIF-1α in our background, but only when we make clear that it comes from the original paper’s mechanism model rather than directly from our own RNA-seq reanalysis.

## What the original paper emphasizes

The original paper emphasizes mechanism. It combines genetic models, pharmacology, neurite outgrowth assays, in vivo regeneration assays, puromycin labeling, and multiple transcriptomic analyses to argue that AhR is a stress-growth switch rather than a classic regeneration-promoting transcription factor ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)). Its RNA-seq interpretation is part of that larger mechanism story, not a stand-alone transcriptome-wide reanalysis paper.

## What our project extends beyond the source paper

Our project uses the same dataset as a transcriptome-wide reanalysis rather than as one component of a broader mechanism paper. That means our strongest contribution is not proving the biology from scratch, but showing how a global differential-expression workflow, bend-point narrowing, and pathway-level follow-up can connect the source paper’s candidate-gene framing to a wider set of reproducible transcriptomic patterns in the side-specific injury contrasts. In practice, that lets us emphasize the hierarchy supported by both the source paper and our weekly reports: side-specific injury contrasts first, genotype as a secondary modifier, and the FF/WT side-specific branch as the clearest pathway-level follow-up in our own pipeline ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)).

## Cautions for reuse in our paper

- Do **not** say our analysis proves the source paper’s mechanism. Our analysis is a reanalysis of the source RNA-seq data, not a replacement for the paper’s functional experiments.
- Do **not** claim that genotype broadly separates all samples. Both the source paper and our own reports support a weaker genotype signal than the injury-side signal ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)).
- Do **not** casually turn pathway enrichment labels into causal statements. The source paper uses multiple functional assays to support its mechanism model; our GO/pathway follow-up is descriptive and hypothesis-generating.
- Be careful with spinal cord injury wording. The source paper includes spinal cord injury models, but our dataset reanalysis is anchored in DRGs collected after peripheral nerve injury, so that distinction should remain explicit ([https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649](https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.11.04.565649)).
- Use `WT` / `cKO` for reader-facing prose when helpful, but preserve `ff` / `cre` where the pipeline or contrast names require the exact internal labels.
